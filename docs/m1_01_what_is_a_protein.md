# 1.1 What Is a Protein?

**Time:** ~30 minutes reading + activity

---

## The Alphabet of Life

Your DNA contains the instructions to build everything in your body. But DNA itself doesn't actually *do* much — it's more like a blueprint stored in a library. The real workers are **proteins**.

A protein is a long chain of smaller molecules called **amino acids**, strung together like beads on a necklace. There are 20 different amino acids — think of them as a 20-letter alphabet. Just as this 20-letter alphabet can spell out every word in the English language, these 20 amino acids can be combined in virtually unlimited ways to build every protein in your body.

> **How many proteins?** Your body has ~20,000 genes, but can produce over 1 million distinct proteins through alternative splicing, modifications, and combinations.

---

## The 20 Amino Acids

Every amino acid has the same core structure, but a different **side chain** — a chemical group that sticks out from the backbone and gives each amino acid its personality.

We can group them by character:

| Group | Examples | Property |
|-------|----------|----------|
| **Hydrophobic** ("water-fearing") | Leucine, Valine, Isoleucine, Phenylalanine | Avoid water — tend to pack into the protein core |
| **Polar uncharged** | Serine, Threonine, Asparagine, Glutamine | Like water, form hydrogen bonds |
| **Positively charged** | Lysine, Arginine, Histidine | Carry a + charge at neutral pH |
| **Negatively charged** | Aspartate, Glutamate | Carry a − charge at neutral pH |
| **Special** | Glycine (tiny, flexible), Proline (rigid, kinks the chain), Cysteine (can form disulfide bonds) | Unique structural roles |

> **Analogy:** Imagine amino acids as LEGO bricks — some are sticky on all sides (hydrophobic), some have magnets (charged), some are tiny connector pieces (glycine). The sequence of bricks determines the shape of the final structure.

---

## From Sequence to Shape to Function

The sequence of amino acids in a protein is called its **primary structure**. But a linear chain of amino acids isn't very useful on its own — it needs to *fold* into a specific 3D shape.

This folding is not random. The sequence determines the shape, and the shape determines the function.

> **Anfinsen's Experiment (1961):** Christian Anfinsen showed that a denatured (unfolded) protein spontaneously refolds into its correct shape when conditions are restored. This proved that **all the information needed to fold is encoded in the sequence itself.** Anfinsen won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1972.

This insight is the foundation of everything we do in this internship — if sequence determines structure, and structure determines function, then **designing a new sequence is designing a new function.**

---

## What Do Proteins Do?

Proteins are the molecular machines of life. They:

- **Catalyze reactions** — enzymes like amylase break down starch in your mouth right now
- **Carry signals** — hormones like insulin are proteins that tell cells to absorb glucose
- **Provide structure** — collagen is the protein scaffold of your skin, tendons, and bones
- **Defend you** — antibodies are proteins that recognize and neutralize pathogens
- **Move things** — motor proteins like kinesin physically walk along cellular tracks carrying cargo
- **Control gene expression** — transcription factors are proteins that turn genes on and off

In this internship, we focus on proteins that **bind other proteins** — specifically, designing a small protein that binds to a cancer-related target to block its activity.

## Activity: Amino Acid Property Map

On a piece of paper (or sticky notes), write out each of the 20 amino acids and color-code them by property:

- 🔴 Red = Hydrophobic
- 🔵 Blue = Polar/hydrophilic
- 🟢 Green = Positively charged
- 🟡 Yellow = Negatively charged
- ⚪ Grey = Special (Gly, Pro, Cys)

Then answer: **If you were designing a protein that needs to hide from water, which amino acids would you put in the core? Which would you put on the surface?**

---

> **Key takeaway:** A protein is a sequence of amino acids that folds into a 3D shape, and that shape determines its function. The 20-amino-acid alphabet can build virtually unlimited different proteins.
