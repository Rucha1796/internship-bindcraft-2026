# 1.5 Antibodies vs. Designed Binders

**Time:** ~25 minutes reading

---

## Two Ways to Block PD-L1

There are two main strategies for blocking PD-L1 therapeutically:

1. **Antibodies** — Large proteins (~150 kDa) produced by the immune system; Keytruda and Opdivo are both antibodies
2. **De novo designed binders** — Small engineered proteins (~7–10 kDa) created from scratch using computational tools like BindCraft

In this internship, you'll design the second type. But it helps to understand why the second approach is worth pursuing when antibodies already work.

---

## Antibodies: What Are They?

Antibodies (also called immunoglobulins) are Y-shaped proteins produced by B cells. They are the immune system's precision targeting system.

### Structure of an Antibody

```
                    Fab (antigen-binding fragment)
                   /          \
          VH — CH1         VL — CL
               |                |
               CH2 ——— CH2
               |                |
               CH3 ——— CH3
                   \          /
                    Fc (constant fragment)
```

- **Variable domain (VH + VL):** The tip of the Y — this is where antigen binding happens
- **CDR loops:** Six loops (3 on VH, 3 on VL) that contact the antigen. H3 (third heavy chain CDR) is the most important and most variable
- **Fc region:** The "stem" of the Y — interacts with immune effector cells and determines the antibody's half-life

Antibodies are ~150,000 Da (150 kDa). For comparison:
- PD-L1 extracellular domain: ~29 kDa
- BindCraft binder (65–90 residues): ~7–10 kDa

### How Antibodies Find Their Target

In nature, antibodies are generated by a process called **somatic hypermutation** — the immune system makes millions of slightly different antibodies and then selects the ones that bind best. This is essentially evolution happening in weeks inside your lymph nodes.

For therapeutic antibodies like Keytruda:
1. Immunize a mouse (or use phage display in a test tube)
2. Select antibodies that bind PD-L1 with high affinity
3. Humanize the antibody (replace mouse framework with human framework to avoid immune reactions)
4. Manufacture at scale in mammalian cell culture
5. Clinical trials → FDA approval

Timeline: typically 5–10 years from antibody selection to approved drug.

## The Limitations of Antibodies

Antibodies work well — but they have real limitations.

| Limitation | Why it matters |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Large size (~150 kDa)** | Poor tumor penetration; can't reach some targets |
| **Complex to manufacture** | Requires mammalian cell culture; expensive |
| **Cannot enter cells** | Antibodies can't cross the cell membrane; restricted to cell-surface targets |
| **Immunogenicity risk** | Body may recognize and attack even humanized antibodies |
| **CDR-limited diversity** | Binding is constrained to loop geometries; can't easily reach flat epitopes |
| **Slow to design** | Generating and humanizing antibodies takes months to years |

## De Novo Designed Binders: What They Are

A **de novo designed binder** is a small protein (typically 50–100 residues) that has been computationally designed from scratch — no natural template — to bind a specific target surface.

BindCraft designs these using AlphaFold2 hallucination. The output is a sequence that has never existed in nature, but folds into a stable structure that should bind the target.

### Advantages Over Antibodies

| Feature | Antibody | Designed Binder |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| Size | ~150 kDa | ~7–10 kDa |
| Design timeline | 1–5 years | Days–weeks (computational) |
| Tumor penetration | Limited | Better (smaller) |
| Manufacturing | Complex (mammalian cells) | Simpler (E. coli) |
| Stability | Moderate (needs cold chain) | Can engineer high stability |
| Target types | Mostly extracellular surface | Same; potentially more flexible |
| Clinical track record | Extensive | Emerging |

### The Cost: Less Clinical Validation

The honest downside of designed binders is that they are a newer technology. Therapeutic antibodies have decades of clinical experience behind them. Designed binders are a rapidly advancing field — several are now in clinical trials — but less is known about how they perform in patients.

## Miniproteins, Affibodies, and Other Scaffolds

Designed binders exist on a spectrum. Some use existing natural protein scaffolds as a starting point:

- **Affibodies** — based on Staphylococcal protein A; small (~7 kDa); used in imaging
- **DARPins** — designed ankyrin repeat proteins; very stable
- **Nanobodies** — single-domain antibody fragments from llamas/sharks (~15 kDa)
- **Miniproteins** — de novo designed from scratch (what BindCraft makes)

BindCraft makes the most computational version: a genuinely *de novo* design with no natural template.

## Why This Internship Uses BindCraft

1. **You start from nothing.** There's no natural binder to copy from. You use computation to find a binder that *should* work.
2. **The design space is enormous.** There are ~20^80 possible 80-residue proteins. BindCraft explores this space by gradient descent on AlphaFold2 predictions.
3. **Fast feedback.** A BindCraft run on a GPU takes hours — not months.
4. **Real science.** Your designed sequences will be synthesized and tested in the wet lab. If they bind, that's a genuine discovery.

> **Key takeaway:** Antibodies are powerful, proven therapeutics — but large, complex, and slow to develop. De novo designed miniprotein binders are smaller, faster to design computationally, and potentially able to reach targets that antibodies can't. BindCraft designs this second type. Both approaches are used in modern drug discovery.
